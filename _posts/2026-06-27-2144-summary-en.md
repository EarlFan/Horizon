## [Why kinetic energy grows with the square of speed, not linearly](https://physics.stackexchange.com/questions/535/why-does-kinetic-energy-increase-quadratically-not-linearly-with-speed) ⭐️ 8.0/10

A 2011 StackExchange question sparked a highly-rated discussion that collected intuitive explanations for why kinetic energy scales quadratically with velocity, using analogies from potential energy and braking distance. The discussion provides rare, non‑mathematical insights into a foundational physics concept, making it valuable for education and for building deeper physical intuition beyond formula memorization. Explanations include: the conversion of potential energy (height squared gives quadruple energy but only double speed), a thought experiment showing a faster car needs more than proportional stopping distance, and a symmetry-based argument akin to Noether’s theorem.

hackernews · ProxyTracer · Jun 26, 22:43 · [Discussion](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48692946)

**Background**: In classical mechanics, kinetic energy is given by E_k = ½mv². This quadratic dependence means that doubling speed requires four times the energy. The result emerges from work‑energy theorem, but its deep origin relates to Galilean invariance and the symmetry of space and time. Potential energy from height (mgh) is linear in height, yet impact speed grows only as the square‑root of height, leading to the observed relationship.

**Discussion**: Commenters largely agree with the intuitive approaches. One user highlights potential energy conversion: a ball from twice the height has double energy but not double speed. Another uses a braking car analogy: a car at 100 units fails to stop in time while one at 70 succeeds, illustrating non‑linear risk. A third notes the difficulty of gaining speed when already moving, and a fourth references a symmetry‑based proof by Ron Maimon that relies on Noether’s theorem.
